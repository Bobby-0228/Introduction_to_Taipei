National Palace Museum

Imperial treasures, 8,000 years of history, and a world-class museum experience.

About the National Palace Museum

Introduction

The National Palace Museum (NPM) in Taipei is home to one of the world’s finest collections of Chinese imperial art and cultural relics. With hundreds of thousands of artifacts spanning over 8,000 years—from Neolithic pottery to masterpieces from the Qing dynasty—the museum offers a deep and beautifully curated journey through Chinese history.

Visitors can explore galleries dedicated to jade, bronzes, ceramics, calligraphy, painting, and more, all housed in a building inspired by traditional Chinese palace architecture.

Historical Background

The National Palace Museum in Taipei traces its origins to the Palace Museum in Beijing, founded in 1925 within the Forbidden City. During the Sino-Japanese War, the most precious parts of the imperial art collection were packed and relocated southward to protect them from wartime damage.

After World War II, the Chinese Civil War prompted another major move. In 1949, a significant portion of the collection—about one-fifth of the original treasures—was transported to Taiwan for safekeeping. These artifacts were first stored in temporary facilities before the construction of a permanent museum in the Waishuangxi area of Taipei.

In 1965, the National Palace Museum officially opened, becoming the long-term home for these invaluable cultural relics. Today, it stands as one of the world’s greatest repositories of Chinese art, shaped profoundly by this extraordinary journey of preservation.

The Three National Treasures

Jadeite Cabbage

The Jadeite Cabbage Carved from a single piece of naturally colored jadeite, the sculpture cleverly incorporates the stone’s white and green hues to resemble a fresh head of Chinese cabbage. The artist skillfully used the material’s natural imperfections—such as color variations and cracks—to form the leaves, stalks, and subtle textures of the vegetable.

Resting on the cabbage are two tiny creatures: a katydid and a locust, both intricately carved. These insects traditionally symbolize fertility and abundance in Chinese culture, leading many scholars to interpret the piece as a metaphor for prosperity and blessings for future generations. Although the exact sculptor and date remain unknown, the piece is often associated with the late Qing dynasty and is believed to have been part of the dowry of Consort Jin, one of the Guangxu Emperor’s wives.

Mao Gong Ding

The Mao Gong Ding is one of the most important bronze ritual vessels from ancient China. Dating to the Western Zhou dynasty (circa 9th century BCE), this massive tripod cauldron was used for ceremonial purposes and is celebrated both for its craftsmanship and its extraordinary inscription.

Cast in bronze with a dignified, symmetrical form, the vessel features two upright handles and three stout legs—hallmarks of Zhou ritual bronzes. Its most remarkable feature is the 500-character inscription cast inside the belly of the vessel. This inscription is the longest known text on any ancient Chinese bronze and serves as a vital historical source. It records the Zhou king’s praise and instructions to a noble named Mao Gong (“Duke Mao”), commending his loyalty and outlining principles of virtuous governance.

Meat-shaped Stone

The Meat-shaped Stone is carved from banded jasper, a type of quartz whose natural layers of red, brown, and ivory tones remarkably resemble the appearance of braised pork belly—a classic Chinese dish. Through careful carving and polishing, the artisan enhanced the stone’s natural striations to mimic the glossy skin, tender layers of fat, and rich texture of slow-cooked Dongpo pork.

The piece dates to the Qing dynasty, though the exact artist remains unknown. Its lifelike realism and playful use of natural materials reflect the Qing court’s appreciation for craftsmanship that blurs the line between art and nature. Historically, the Meat-shaped Stone was part of the collection of the Qing imperial household and was treasured for its whimsical charm and technical virtuosity.

Visiting Tips

How to Make the Most of Your Visit

The collection is huge, and it’s impossible to see everything in one go. For a first-time visit, focus on a few key areas such as jade, bronzes, and the “Three Treasures” section. The museum also offers audio guides and guided tours in multiple languages.

For a more relaxed experience, visit on weekday mornings or later in the afternoon. You can also combine your trip with Shilin Night Market in the evening for food and street life.

Practical Information

  • Location: Shilin District, Taipei
  • Suggested Time: 2–3 hours (more if you love art & history)
  • Tickets: Discounts are often available for students and combo tickets with the Southern Branch.
  • Facilities: Lockers, café, gift shop, and a scenic outdoor area.
